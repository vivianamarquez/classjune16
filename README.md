## The Cat Who Learned to Wait

In a small apartment above a bakery, a gray tabby named Miso spent her mornings watching the world through a sun-warmed window. She was not the loudest cat on the block, nor the fastest, but she was patient in a way that made other animals uneasy.

Every day at dawn, the baker downstairs slid trays of bread into the oven. The smell rose through the floorboards like a gentle alarm. Miso would stretch, blink once, and take her post on the windowsill. She was waiting for the sparrow who lived in the gutter across the street.

The sparrow was bold. It hopped along the ledge, pecked at crumbs, and never seemed afraid of anything—except, perhaps, of being bored. Miso never chased it. She had tried once, long ago, and the sparrow had simply flown to the next roof, unimpressed. After that, Miso decided that chasing was a poor strategy. Watching was better.

Weeks passed. Rain came. Leaves fell. The sparrow brought a friend, then another. Miso watched them all with the calm attention of someone reading a book she already knew by heart.

One cold evening, the bakery closed early. The street went quiet. Miso heard a faint tapping at the window—not wings, but something smaller and more desperate. A kitten, soaked and shivering, had climbed the fire escape and pressed its nose against the glass.

Miso looked at the kitten. She looked at the sparrow, who had tucked itself under the awning for the night. Then she did something she had never done before: she meowed—not at the sparrow, but at her human, loud and clear, until the window opened.

The kitten came inside. The human dried its fur and set out a second bowl of food. Miso ate beside the newcomer without complaint. When the kitten finally slept, curled into a tight comma on the rug, Miso returned to her windowsill.

The sparrow was still there in the morning, bold as ever. Miso blinked at it. The sparrow chirped once, as if to say, *Well done.*

Miso did not chase. She did not need to. She had learned that some things are worth waiting for, and some arrivals are worth announcing—especially when they arrive small, wet, and in need of a warm place to land.

That is the story of Miso, the cat who learned to wait, and who, in the end, knew exactly when to speak up.
